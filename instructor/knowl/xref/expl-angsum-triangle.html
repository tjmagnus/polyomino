Exploration 2.2.2.

The GeoGebra app, Figure 2.2.10 is useful in completing this activity. You may also perform this activity by carefully cutting out a scalene triangle and tracing it on a sheet of paper.
(a)
Rotate triangle \(\Delta ABC\) 180 degrees about the midpoint \(D\) of side \(\overline{BC}\) (see hint). Both the original triangle and the rotated version should be visible. What do you notice?
Note that two of the points are double labeled, once for each triangle. If you rightclick on the label, you can move it to make the original label visible.
Hint.
To perform this rotation in the GeoGebra app, click on the angle rotation icon in the toolbar. Next, click on the interior of the triangle to select the object to be rotated, then click on midpoint \(D\) to identify the center of the rotation. When prompted to input the angle measure, replace 45 with 180. Then click OK.
Answer.
The rotated triangle, \(\Delta A'B'C'\text{,}\) perfectly overlaps with the original triangle, but flipped. See "Solution" for a list of possible observations.
Solution.
Possible Observations: The two triangles are identical in size and shape, they share a common side \(\overline{BC}\text{,}\) and the combined shape formed by both triangles is a parallelogram.
(b)
Rotate \(\Delta ABC\) 180 degrees about the midpoint \(E\) of side \(\overline{AB}\text{.}\) Which angle of \(\Delta ABC\) is congruent to \(\angle A'C'B'\text{?}\)
Hint.
Think about where \(\angle C\) rotates when the triangle is flipped around the midpoint of \(\overline{AB}\text{.}\)
Answer.
\(\angle A'C'B'\) is congruent to \(\angle ACB\text{.}\)
Solution.
Rotating around midpoint \(E\) maps vertex \(C\) to a new location \(C'\) on the opposite side of \(\overline{AB}\text{.}\) This rotation preserves angle measures, \(\angle A'C'B'\) corresponds to the original \(\angle ACB\) in both shape and size.
(c)
Rotate \(\Delta ABC\) 180 degrees about the midpoint \(E\) of side \(\overline{AB}\text{.}\) Which angle of \(\Delta ABC\) is congruent to \(\angle B_{1}'A_{1}'C_{1}'\text{?}\)
Hint.
Consider the rules of corresponding angles. Recall that alternate interior angles are always congruent. Which is the alternate interior angle to \(\angle B_{1}'A_{1}'C_{1}'\text{?}\)
Answer.
Angle \(\angle ABC\text{.}\)
Solution.
When \(\Delta ABC\) is rotated 180 degrees around midpoint \(E\) of side \(\overline{AB}\text{,}\) a new triangle \(\Delta A'B'C'\) is formed. Both \(\angle ABC\) (original triangle) and \(\angle B'A'C'\) (new triangle) are interior angles. Therefore, these angles are congruent.
(d)
What does this tell you about \(m\angle ABC+m\angle BCA + m\angle CAB\text{?}\) Explain how the picture shows this.
Hint.
Recall the Interior Angles Theorem. What is always the sum of the three interior angles of a triangle?
Answer.
The sum of the interior angles of a triangle is 180 degrees.
Solution.
When the triangle is rotated around each side, the interior angles line up to form a straight line. In geometry, a straight line always measures 180 degrees. Therefore, the three angles inside the triangle must also add up to 180 degrees.
(e)
Make a claim about the sum of the interior angles of a triangle.
Does your claim work for all triangles or just the one in the picture? To test this in the GeoGebra applet, select the arrow in the tool menu. Then click and drag one of the vertices in the original triangle to change the shape. Do your claims still hold?
Hint.
Test this claim by changing the triangle’s shape in GeoGebra or by rotating the triangle as instructed.
Answer.
The sum of the interior angles of any triangle is always 180 degrees, regardless of its shape.
Solution.
By rotating the triangle around the midpoints and observing how angles line up, it becomes clear that the three interior angles together form a straight line where their sum is 180°. This holds true even with changing the triangle’s size or shape in GeoGebra.
GeoGebra makes exploring multiple possibilities easier. If you are doing this activity with paper triangles, experiment with other triangles, repeating the previous tasks. If your classmates used different triangles, this would be a good time to compare results.
