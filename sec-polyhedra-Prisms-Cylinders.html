    Section 3.3 Prisms and Cylinders

    Subsection 3.3.1 Definitions

    Let us explore the geometric solid known as a prism in greater detail. As noted in Definition 3.2.7, a prism is a box-like geometric solid with a pair of congruent and parallel polygonal faces, the base and the summit, joined by a band of parallelograms. The number of parallelograms connecting the bases will always equal the number of sides of a base.
    We are most familiar with right prisms, namely prisms where the bases meet all the side faces at right angles; the side faces of a right prism are rectangles. An oblique prism is a prism with at least one side face is not a rectangle.
    Although a cylinder is not a polyhedron since it has a circular base, we will see that there are some similarities between prisms and cylinders. Both have parallel, congruent bases.

    Checkpoint 3.3.3. Check your understanding.

    Besides the parallel circles, what other shape(s) is needed to form a cylinder? How many of these shapes are there?
    Hint.
    Cut out pieces of paper to cover a cylindrical can. In addition to the two circles, what else do you need?
    An important measurement for prisms and cylinders is the distance between the bases. Many geometry textbooks use the term height to describe this distance.

    Definition 3.3.4.

    The height of a prism or cylinder is the distance between the parallel congruent bases and is measured along a line perpendicular to the bases. When a prism or cylinder is sitting on a side other than a base, the height will be measured laterally instead of vertically. In fact, this text will use the word stretch in lieu of ‘height’ where confusion may occur.
    The height or stretch of a right prism is usually measured along an edge of one of the rectangular sides, like \(d\) in Figure 3.3.1.(a). The stretch of a skew prism, on the other hand, will be less than the height of a side face as illustrated in Figure 3.3.1.(b).

    Subsection 3.3.2 Volumes of Prisms and Cylinders

    In this exploration, we develop learn how to measure the space inside of a prism or pyramid. Hopefully, exploring the meaning of volume will help you appreciate and remember how to find volume.

    Exploration 3.3.1. What is Volume.

    (a)
    Suppose you have a box (right rectangular prism) that has a height (or stretch) of 6 inches, a length of 5 inches, and a width of 7 inches and you have a large supply of \(1''\times 1''\times 1''\)-cubes.
    1. If you cover the bottom of the inside of the box with a single layer of \(1''\times 1''\times 1''\)-cubes, how many cubes will fit in this first layer?
    2. How many horizontal layers of \(1''\times 1''\times 1''\)-cubes could you fit in the box? Would each of these layers contain the same number of cubes as the first layer?
    3. What is the total number of 1”x1”x1”-cubes that could fit in the box?
    (b)
    Explain why the formula for the volume of a rectangular box is \(V=\ell wh\) where \(\ell\) is the height, \(w\) is the width, and \(h\) is the height of the box.
    (c)
    When \(h\) is a whole number, it makes sense to think about the height as the number of layers. Not all measurements are whole numbers though. Instead we can think of \(h\) as the amount that we need to stretch that first layer to reach the summit of the prism. Suppose that our rectangular prism has the dimensions, \(\ell=5\) inches, \(w=6\) inches, and a stretch of \(d=2.5\) inches. Find the volume of the prism.
    Here you may be imagining two layers with a half layer on top of them. The half cubes (\(1''\times 1''\times 0.5''\)-cubes on the top layer can be paired to create full \(1''\times 1''\times 1''\)-cubes.
    (d)
    This notion of stretching the bottom layer holds for all prisms and cylinders, not just rectangular ones. Suppose a cylinder has a circular base with area 9.4 square centimeters.
    1. If the cylinder is one centimeter tall, what is the volume of the cylinder?
    2. If the cylinder is two centimeters tall, what is the volume?
    3. If the cylinder is five centimeters tall, what is the volume?
    4. If the cylinder is 7.5 centimeters tall, what is the volume?
    5. If the cylinder is \(d\) centimeters tall, what is volume?
    Exploration What is Volume showed that the the volume of a prism or cylinder was equal to the area of the base times the number of horizontal layers. While you may be used to the formula \(V=\ell wh\text{,}\) this formula only works for rectangular prisms. Instead, note that this formula \(V=(\ell w)d\) is equivalent to the formula \(V=Ad\) where \(A=\ell w\) is the area of one base of a rectangular prism and \(d\) is the distance between the bases, measured perpendicular to the bases. This volume formula \(V=Ad\) will extend to ALL prisms and even cylinders! Much less to memorize! We only need to compute the area of the base and then multiply the area by the distance between the bases.

    Subsection 3.3.3 The Surface Area of Prisms and Cylinders

    While the volume measures the amount of interior space of a 3-dimensional solid, the surface area measures the amount of material needed to create the shell or surface of the solid. To compute surface area, we simply compute the area of each face and add the areas together.

    Exploration 3.3.3. Finding the Surface Area of Prisms and Cylinders.

    (a)
    The triangular prism in Figure 3.3.5 has five faces. Determine the area of each face and then compute the surface area.
    (b)
    The right cylinder in Figure 3.3.7 has a height of 20 feet and a diameter of 10 ft. Determine the volume of each face and then compute the surface area.
    (c)
    Create a formula for a right cylinder with height \(h\) where the radius of each circular base is \(r\text{.}\) Your formula should contain the variables \(h\) and \(r\text{.}\) Then check that your formula gives the same result as you found in Task 3.3.3.b.
