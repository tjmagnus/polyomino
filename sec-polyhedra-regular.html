    Section 3.1 Regular Polyhedra

    Subsection 3.1.1 Definitions

    In this section we will explore three-dimensional solids. Solids with flat polygonal sides, like cubes and pyramids, are called polyhedra.

    Definition 3.1.1.

    A polyhedron (plural: polyhedra) is a three-dimensional closed figure in which all of the faces are polygons.
    Here, a face of a geometrical solid refers to a flat surface that forms part of the boundary of the solid. The line segments where two faces meet are called edges, and the ‘corners’ where edges meet are called the vertices of the polyhedron. The vertices of the polyhedron are also vertices of polygonal faces. Unless specified otherwise, we will be measuring the angles formed by a pair of edges which is the same as measuring the angle of a vertex of a polygonal face.

    Checkpoint 3.1.2.

    Which of the following solids are polyhedra?
    1. cylinder
    2. cube
    3. triangular prism
    4. sphere
    In this section, we define and explore regular polyhedra. These special solids appear in ancient art work, were identified by Greek mathematicians around 300 B.C., and are used to model chemical and biological structures today. We will find that only there are only a few regular polyhedra.

    Definition 3.1.3.

    A polyhedron is said to be regular if it satisfies these two conditions:
    • Every face is the same regular polygon and
    • At every vertex, the number of polygons is the same.
