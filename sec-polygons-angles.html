    Section 2.2 Angle Measure

    Subsection 2.2.1 A Tool for Measuring Angles

    To begin our study of angle measure, we will adopt the practice of assuming that there are 360 degrees in a full rotation about any point. The choice of 360 can be traced back to the Babylonians who were studying planetory paths around 400 B.C. and used a base 60 (instead of base 10) number system. One advantage of 360 is that it has a lot of factors, including 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 8.
    Suppose point \(A\) lies between points \(B\) and \(C\) on line \(\overleftrightarrow{BC}\text{.}\) Since \(\frac{360}{2}=180\text{,}\) a rotation of 180 degrees will take the ray \(\overrightarrow{AB}\) to the ray \(\overrightarrow{AC}\) on the opposite side of \(A\text{.}\)
    We can continue to define the measurement of angles according to what fraction of a full (or half) rotation they represent. Notice how the protractor divides the semicircular arc into 180 segments, eighteen of which are labeled with numerical values. These are multiples of ten, representing \(\frac{1}{18}\) of the half-rotation. To find the measure of \(\angle{DAB}\text{,}\) we place the center dot of the protractor over the vertex \(A\) of the angle and lined up ray \(\overrightarrow{AB}\) with the mark for 0 degrees. The other side of the angle \(\overrightarrow{AD}\) passes through the label for 40 degrees.

    Subsection 2.2.3 Additivity of Angles and Definitions Associated with Angles

    In this section, you have likely been assuming the additivity of angle measure. Like the additivity of area¬†1.1.3 and length¬†2.0.1, this is intuitively obvious and will prove useful. We state this as a principle and add it to our toolbox.

    Principle 2.2.4.

    If ray \(\overrightarrow{AD}\) lies in the interior of angle \(\angle BAC\text{,}\) then \(m\angle{BAC}=m\angle{BAD}+m\angle{DAC}\text{.}\)
    Some new terminology and notation have also been introduced in this section.

    Definition 2.2.5.

    A line is a one-dimensional figure consisting of a set of points. It does not have any thickness. Because a line extends infinitely in both directions and has no endpoints, we cannot measure the length of a line. For any two distinct points, there is exactly one line that contains both of them so we may refer to a line by a pair of points on the line, \(\overleftrightarrow{AB}\) or by a single lower case letter such as \(\ell\) or \(m\text{.}\)
    When we talk about measuring length, we are referring to a line segment. A line segment is a part of a line consisting of a pair of endpoints and all of the points on the line between the two endpoints. For example, line segment \(\overline{AB}\) consists of points \(A\) and \(B\) and all points on line \(\overleftrightarrow{AB}\) between \(A\) and \(B\text{.}\)
    In a similar way, we define ray \(\overrightarrow{AB}\) to consist of point \(A\text{,}\) point \(B\text{,}\) and all points on line \(\overleftrightarrow{AB}\) which are on the same side of \(A\) as point \(B\text{.}\) A ray extends infinitely in one direction, has a single endpoint, and cannot be measured.

    Definition 2.2.6.

    An angle is the union of two rays with a common endpoint. In particular, \(\angle ABC\) consists of all points that lie on the rays \(\overrightarrow{AB}\) and \(\overrightarrow{AB}\text{.}\) The common endpoint is called the vertex of the angle and the two rays are called the sides of the angle.
    When we measure an angle, denoted \(m\angle ABC\text{,}\) we are measuring how far we would need to rotate one side of the angle around the vertex to meet the other side. This definition introduces some ambiguity as one might rotate clockwise or counterclockwise and we could even rotate multiple times around the vertex. Typically, we will consider the measure of an angle to be a number between 0 and 180 but there will be some exceptions. When we study symmetry and isometry later in Chapter¬†4, we will allow a wider variety of rotations. In addition, we might speak of the interior of some angles of a concave polygon as having at least one interior angle with a measure between 180 and 360 degrees.

    Subsection 2.2.4 Vertical Angles

    To conclude this section, we will use what we have learned to make a claim about vertical angles.

    Definition 2.2.7.

    When a pair of lines intersect, each pair of opposite angles formed by the intersection are said to be a pair of vertical angles. Vertical angles will have a common vertex, namely the point of intersection, and the sides of one angle will be the opposite rays of the sides of the other angle.
