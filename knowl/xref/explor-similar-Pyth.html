Exploration 5.3.2. A Proof of the Pythagorean Theorem.

Use Figure 5.3.3 for this exploration:
(a) Triangles within a triangle.
  1. Measure angles \(\angle ABC\text{,}\) \(\angle BCA\text{,}\) and \(\angle CAB\text{.}\)
  2. Draw a line segment from point \(C\) meeting side \(\overline{AB}\) perpendicularly at a point \(D\text{.}\) How many triangles are now in the sketch? Name them.
  3. To what other angle(s) is \(\angle CDA\) congruent?
  4. To what other angle(s) is \(\angle ACD\) congruent?
  5. Explain how you know triangle \(\Delta ACD\) is similar to \(\Delta ABC\text{.}\) What is the constant of proportionality?
  6. What other triangle is similar to triangle \(\Delta ABC\text{?}\) Be sure to write the letters in the order of corresponding vertices.
(b) A Similar Triangle Proof of the Pythagorean Theorem.
We continue to use Figure 5.3.3 in this task.
  1. Explain why \(\frac{AC}{AB}=\frac{AD}{AC}\) and \(\frac{BC}{AB}=\frac{BD}{BC}\text{.}\)
  2. Since \(\frac{AC}{AB}=\frac{AD}{AC}\text{,}\) we see \(AC\cdot AC=AB\cdot AD\text{.}\) Why?
  3. Use the equation\(\frac{BC}{AB}=\frac{BD}{BC}\text{,}\) to complete the equation \(BC\cdot BC=\text{.}\)
  4. So that the Pythagorean Theorem will be more obvious, we will assign each length a single letter; namely, \(a=BC\text{,}\) \(b=AC\text{,}\) and \(c=/AB\text{.}\) What is \(AD+DB\) using these lower-case letter(s)?
  5. Equating \(a^2+b^2\) with \(BC\cdot BC+AC\cdot AC\text{,}\) substitute the righthand expressions from the equations in the first two parts of this task. Then add the expressions. Do you get \(c^2\text{?}\) Show all work.
